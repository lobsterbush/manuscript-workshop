Motivation

The hidden curriculum

Publishing is one of the most important skills in academia. It is also one of the least taught.

  • Graduate programs teach theory and methods — rarely how to navigate the publishing process
  • Journal selection is learned through trial and error (often painful)
  • Rejection is universal — but almost never discussed openly
  • Formatting and presentation are skills with enormous returns on investment

Publishing is learned through experience, not coursework. This workshop gives you tools to handle the full cycle.

Part 1

Understanding Rankings
& Strategies

Where you submit matters as much as what you submit.

Rankings

Why journal choice matters

The wrong journal wastes months. The right journal changes careers.

  • Audience — your paper reaches the people who care about it
  • Credibility — peer review at a respected venue signals quality
  • Speed — good fit reduces desk rejection and review time
  • Career impact — tenure committees, grant panels, and collaborators all notice where you publish

Common mistake: Defaulting to the highest-ranked journal without considering fit. A well-placed article in a specialist journal that reaches the right audience will often accumulate more citations — and more impact — than a poorly-fitted article in a generalist.

Rankings

Journal metrics: a field guide

Impact Factor

Average citations over 2 years. From Clarivate/Web of Science. Most widely cited, but favours certain fields.

SJR / Scopus

Weighted citations over 3 years. Considers prestige of citing journal. Broader coverage than JCR.

h5-index

Google Scholar metric: h papers with h+ citations in 5 years. Good for emerging or interdisciplinary journals.

Quartile Ranking

Q1–Q4 based on Impact Factor or SJR within a field. Q1 = top 25%. Quick shorthand for journal standing.

CiteScore

Scopus metric: citations over 4 years divided by documents. Wider publication window than Impact Factor.

FWCI

Field-Weighted Citation Impact. Normalises citations by discipline, year, and document type. 1.0 = world average.

Key insight: Metrics vary wildly between disciplines. Only compare journals within the same field. A 2.0 Impact Factor is excellent in some subfields and mediocre in others.

Rankings

Beyond the numbers

Metrics tell part of the story. These factors matter just as much:

  • Scope and audience — Does the journal publish work like yours? Who reads it?
  • Turnaround time — Median days from submission to first decision varies enormously (30 to 300+)
  • Acceptance rate — Top generalists reject 90–95%. Field journals: 70–85%
  • Open access options — Does it offer OA? At what cost? Does your institution have an agreement?
  • Reputation among peers — Ask your advisors and co-authors where they'd send this paper
  • Open calls for papers — Special issues can be excellent fits, but vet the editors and timeline carefully

Strategy

Generalist vs. specialist journals

Generalist (discipline-wide)

  • Higher visibility across the discipline
  • Lower acceptance (5–10%)
  • Broader audience but harder to match reviewers
  • Examples: the flagship journal in your discipline

Specialist (subfield/topic)

  • Expert audience who deeply engage
  • Higher acceptance (15–30%)
  • Faster reviews from specialists
  • Examples: the top journal for your subfield or method

Neither is inherently better. Match the journal to the paper's contribution. A methodological advance belongs in a methods journal. A finding about foreign policy belongs in an international relations journal.

Strategy

Building a target journal list

Before you write, identify 3–5 journals. Rank them in order of preference.

  1. Check your references — Where are the papers you cite published? Those journals are your natural homes.
  2. Ask mentors and co-authors — "Where would you send this?" is the single most useful question.
  3. Read recent issues — Skim the last 2–3 issues. Does your paper fit the conversation?
  4. Check metrics and speed — SCImago, JCR, Google Scholar Metrics
  5. Verify legitimacy — Is it indexed in Scopus/WoS? Beware predatory journals (use Cabells or Think.Check.Submit)

Strategy

The submission ladder

Many scholars follow a "ladder" strategy:

1 Aspirational journal — your top choice. May desk reject, but worth trying.
2 Strong match — excellent fit, reasonable acceptance rate.
3 Good alternative — solid journal, perhaps broader scope.
4 Safety option — newer or niche journal that regularly publishes your type of work.

Critical rule: Submit to only one journal at a time. Dual submission is an ethical violation that can result in being blacklisted.

Part 2

Writing for the
Selected Journal

A manuscript written for one journal rarely fits another without adaptation.

Writing

Read the journal before you write for it

This is the single most common piece of advice from editors — and the most commonly ignored.

  • Read 5–10 recent articles in the journal, especially those close to your topic
  • Note structure — Do they use subheadings? How long are introductions? Where do hypotheses appear?
  • Note methods — What methods predominate? What evidence standards apply?
  • Note citation patterns — Which bodies of literature do published papers engage with?
  • Note length — Word limits vary dramatically (3,000 to 12,000+)

Example: A specialist field journal may expect 10,000 words with deep engagement with the subfield literature. A high-profile interdisciplinary journal may cap you at 4,000 words and demand broad framing for a general audience. Same data, completely different manuscripts.

Writing

Identifying the gap

Every publishable paper addresses a gap. Name yours explicitly.

Evidence

Contradictory findings that need resolution

Knowledge

A phenomenon not yet studied

Practical

Disconnect between research and practice

Methodological

Current methods are inadequate

Empirical

Theory lacks empirical verification

Theoretical

Theory cannot explain observations

Population

Underrepresented groups or regions

Your introduction should name the gap clearly. Reviewers want to know in the first two pages what’s missing and why your paper fills it.

Adapted from Miles (2017)

Writing

Framing your contribution

Your paper should offer at least one form of novelty:

Theoretical

  • Invent a new concept
  • Synthesise existing concepts in new ways
  • Test different theories / triangulation

Methodological

  • Mixed or multi-methods
  • Longitudinal research designs
  • New analytical approaches

Empirical

  • New contexts, regions, or cases
  • Data from understudied populations
  • New evidence (big data, archives, etc.)

State your novelty in the introduction. Reviewers should know within the first two pages what makes this paper new.

Source: Sovacool et al. (2018)

Writing

Types of academic prose

Published papers move beyond description. Know where your writing sits:

1
Descriptive

Presents facts or information. A summary of findings or a report of results.

2
Analytical

Reorganises facts into categories, patterns, and relationships defined by your discipline.

3
Persuasive

Adds your own point of view. Each claim is supported by evidence. Drives the discussion and conclusion.

4
Critical

Considers multiple viewpoints, evaluates merits, identifies strengths and weaknesses. The highest level.

Aim for analytical and critical prose. Pure description is necessary in methods and results, but your discussion must be critical.

Writing

Manuscript structure: the standard template

Most social science journals expect some version of this:

  1. Title — concise, descriptive, searchable
  2. Abstract — 150–250 words; question, method, finding
  3. Introduction — puzzle, contribution, roadmap
  4. Literature / Theory — what we know, what's missing
  5. Research design — data, methods, identification
  1. Results — main findings, robustness checks
  2. Discussion — what this means
  3. Conclusion — implications, limitations, future research
  4. References — every citation, correctly formatted
  5. Appendices — supplemental analyses, additional tables

Writing

The introduction: your 90-second pitch

Reviewers decide their initial impression in the first two pages. Make them count.

1 The puzzle — What is the question? Why does it matter? Open with the research question.
2 The gap — What do we not know? What has previous research missed?
3 The contribution — What does this paper add? Be specific.
4 The preview — How will you answer the question? Brief roadmap.

Tip: Write the introduction last. You can't pitch the paper convincingly until you know exactly what you've found.

Writing

Title and abstract: your paper's front door

Most readers will only ever see these. Editors use them to select reviewers. Search engines index them.

Title

  • Concise and descriptive
  • Include key variables or concepts
  • Avoid jargon that limits discoverability
  • Consider including the finding ("X increases Y" vs. "A study of X")

Abstract (5 elements)

  1. The problem — context and hook
  2. The purpose — research question
  3. The method — how the study was done
  4. The finding — key results
  5. The significance — what it means

The 4Cs: A good abstract is Clear, Concise, Convincing, and Cohesive. Every word should earn its place.

Writing

The cover letter

Short, professional, and purposeful. Editors read hundreds — make yours count.

Paragraph 1: Title, authors, what the paper does (2–3 sentences).
Paragraph 2: Why this journal — connect your contribution to the journal's scope and recent conversation.
Paragraph 3: Any required declarations — not previously published, not under review elsewhere, ethics approval, data availability.
Optional: Suggest 2–5 reviewers (some journals require this, some don't allow it). Choose scholars who know the field but aren't close collaborators.

Common mistake: Sending the same generic cover letter to every journal. Editors notice immediately.

Writing

Anonymization for blind review

Most social science journals use double-blind review. Anonymization mistakes cause desk rejection.

  • Remove author names from the manuscript body, headers, footers
  • Remove identifying self-citations — replace "As we showed in Smith (2024)" with "As shown in [Author] (2024)"
  • Check document properties — Word/PDF metadata may contain your name
  • Separate title page — author info, affiliations, acknowledgments on a separate file
  • Check supplementary materials — code files, data documentation may reveal identity

Writing

Revision and proofreading

Writing is rewriting. Build revision into your process, not the end.

  • Take a break — even one day away gives you fresh eyes. Distance improves judgment.
  • Check coherence — does every paragraph serve the argument? Cut what doesn’t.
  • Check adherence to guidelines — word count, formatting, citation style.
  • Proofread for grammar and typos — these signal carelessness to reviewers.
  • Read aloud — awkward sentences reveal themselves when spoken.

Pro tip: Get a colleague to read your paper before submission. Fresh readers catch what you’ve become blind to.

Writing

Common formatting pitfalls

References

Wrong citation style. Missing DOIs. Cited but not listed (or vice versa). Use a reference manager.

Word count

Over the limit = instant desk rejection at many journals. Count carefully. Some include references, some don't.

Figures & tables

Low resolution (need 300+ DPI). Not numbered sequentially. Placed incorrectly. Check if the journal wants them inline or at the end.

Pro tip: Read the author guidelines fully before your final formatting pass. Many journals now accept "free format" submissions — but read carefully to confirm.

Part 3

The Submission
Process

From "submit" to decision — what actually happens.

Submission

Before you hit submit

A pre-submission checklist saves heartache:

  • All co-authors have read and approved the manuscript
  • Author guidelines followed — format, word count, reference style, figure specs
  • Properly anonymized — names, self-citations, metadata
  • Ethics statement included if human subjects research
  • Data availability statement — increasingly required
  • Cover letter tailored to this specific journal
  • Supplementary materials uploaded separately if required

Submission

Authorship and attribution

Agree on authorship before you start writing, not after.

Who qualifies as an author?

  • Significant contribution to conception and design
  • Non-routine data collection requiring intellectual input
  • Analysis or interpretation of data
  • Drafting or critical revision of the manuscript

Key principles

  • No automatic authorship for supervisors
  • Use written authorship agreements
  • All co-authors must approve the final manuscript
  • Know your institution’s policy and dispute resolution process

Have the conversation early. Authorship disputes are among the most common and damaging conflicts in academia. A written agreement prevents most of them.

Submission

What happens after you submit

Submit
Assign editor
Triage
Peer review
Editor assesses
Decision
1 Editorial triage — Editor-in-chief checks scope and quality. 25–60% of papers are desk rejected at this stage. Days to weeks.
2 Reviewer assignment — Editor selects 2–3 reviewers. This can take weeks (finding willing reviewers is increasingly hard).
3 Peer review — Reviewers evaluate methodology, contribution, writing. Usually 4–12 weeks.
4 Decision — Accept (rare), revise & resubmit, or reject.

Submission

Types of decisions

Accept

Extremely rare on first submission. Celebrate accordingly.

Revise & Resubmit

This is good news. The journal sees potential. Major R&R vs. minor R&R: both mean the door is open.

Reject

With or without review. The most common outcome. It is not the end.

An R&R is not a rejection. Strategically revised R&R papers have high acceptance rates. Treat it as an investment, not a burden.

Submission

What good reviewers do

Understanding reviewer expectations helps you write for them — and interpret their feedback.

  • Assess novelty, rigor, and style — judge the paper on its own terms
  • Embrace interdisciplinarity — avoid disciplinary dogma
  • Be constructive — collegiate language, actionable suggestions
  • Respect editorial screens — defer to the editor on journal fit
  • Commit to reviewing revisions — follow through on the full cycle
  • Meet deadlines — delays cascade through the entire process

Becoming a reviewer makes you a better author. You learn to see manuscripts the way editors do. Volunteer to review when opportunities arise.

Based on Sovacool et al. (2022)

Part 4

Dealing with
Rejections

The part no one talks about — but everyone experiences.

Rejection

Rejection is normal

85%

of rejected papers eventually publish elsewhere

90%+

of top generalist journals reject submissions

An article of mine was rejected eight times before being published. — Political science professor, quoted in the literature on academic resilience. This is not unusual.

Rejection

The shadow CV

Behind every published paper is a trail of rejections you don't see.

  • That article in a top journal? Rejected three times before.
  • That grant? Countless revisions and rejections.
  • Your advisor's CV? Full of invisible failures.

The difference between published and unpublished scholars is not talent. It's persistence.

Rejection

Types of rejection

Desk rejection

Without peer review. Usually: scope mismatch, formatting errors, or quality below threshold. Often fast (days to weeks). Don't take it personally — try to learn what went wrong.

Rejection after review

Reviewers identified substantive issues. This is valuable feedback. Read it carefully (after a few days). Use it to improve before resubmitting elsewhere.

Possible reasons for desk rejection: wrong scope, poor English, inappropriate methodology, word count violation, failed anonymization. All of these are fixable.

Rejection

Three strategic options after rejection

1
Resubmit unchanged

When: reviewers misunderstood your project or wanted inappropriate changes. Risk: same reviewer may see it again, especially in small fields.

2
Make selective revisions ← Usually best

Address genuine weaknesses that multiple reviewers flagged. Ignore peripheral preferences. Distinguish real problems from individual taste.

3
Address everything

Caution: next journal = different reviewers = different preferences. Overfitting to one set of comments produces incoherent work that satisfies no one.

Rejection

The revision checklist

When you get an R&R (or decide to revise after rejection), work systematically:

1
Theme

Group reviewer comments by topic. Identify overlapping concerns across reviewers.

2
Prioritise

Address structural and methodological concerns first. Minor wording suggestions last.

3
Set deadlines

Time-bound each revision task. Most journals expect R&R turnaround within 3–6 months.

Details matter in resubmission: reference formatting, punctuation, capitalisation, table/figure formatting. Follow the journal guidelines precisely.

Rejection

Responding to reviewers

Your response letter is as important as your revised manuscript. Tone matters.

Good responses

  • “We thank the reviewer for this suggestion…”
  • “The reviewer correctly pointed out that…”
  • “We acknowledge this limitation. We have addressed it by…”
  • “We concur with Reviewer #1; nonetheless…”

Poor responses

  • “The reviewers do not understand our point…”
  • “It would not be necessary to change…”
  • “We simply do not have such data…”
  • “Repeating the analysis would not change anything…”

Even when you disagree, be respectful. Explain your reasoning clearly. Editors notice tone — and a constructive disagreement can strengthen your paper.

Rejection

The emotional reality

Rejection feels personal. It isn't — but that doesn't make it hurt less.

  1. Read the decision letter. Then put it away. What seems devastating today will feel manageable in a few days.
  2. Allow yourself to feel frustrated. You're human. This is normal. Don't pretend it doesn't sting.
  3. After a few days, reread strategically. Which comments recur? Which point to genuine weaknesses?
  4. Separate the paper from yourself. Rejection of your manuscript is not rejection of you.
  5. Talk to someone. Advisors, colleagues, peers — almost everyone has been through this. Share it.

Rejection

When to persist vs. when to let go

Keep going when:

  • Reviewers disagree with each other
  • Feedback is about framing, not substance
  • You have a plan to address the weaknesses
  • You still believe in the core question

Consider stopping when:

  • Multiple reviewers across journals identify the same methodological flaw
  • The data cannot support the claims
  • You no longer believe the finding is valid
  • 3+ rejections with converging feedback

Letting go is not failure. It's judgment. Some of the best scholars know when to move on to the next project.

Warning

A word on predatory journals

When you're frustrated with rejections, beware the easy path.

  • Red flags: unsolicited email invitations, guaranteed fast acceptance, vague scope, suspiciously low fees
  • Check: Is the journal indexed in Scopus or Web of Science?
  • Use: Think.Check.Submit — a free checklist to evaluate journals
  • Ask: Have respected scholars in your field published there?

Publishing in a predatory journal can harm your reputation more than not publishing at all. Patience with legitimate journals is always the better strategy.

Resilience

Building a publishing practice

Publishing is not a single event. It's a practice, like writing itself.

  • Have multiple projects — when one paper is under review, work on another. Don't wait.
  • Keep a submission log — track where you submitted, when, decisions received, and next steps.
  • Build a review habit — reviewing for journals makes you a better writer and builds professional relationships.
  • Find a writing group — regular accountability and peer feedback before submission.
  • Read widely — reading good writing makes you a better writer.

Summary &
Principles

Summary

What makes a publishable paper

↑ A clear question
Answerable, important, and specific
↑ A genuine contribution
Something new — theory, method, or evidence
↑ Rigorous methods
Appropriate design, transparent analysis
↑ Journal fit
Right audience, right conversation
↑ Clear writing
Accessible, concise, well-structured
↑ Persistence
Strategic revision and resubmission

Principles

Five principles to take home

  1. Choose the journal first. Write for the audience, not the other way around.
  2. Read the journal. Understand its norms before you submit.
  3. Invest in the cover letter. Show the editor why your paper belongs in their journal.
  4. Use rejection strategically. Free feedback from experts. Take what improves the paper.
  5. Keep going. The difference between published and unpublished is persistence, not brilliance.
